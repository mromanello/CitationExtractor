At R.
462 A-E, Plato provides an account of political unity summarized in the following claims : 1) Political unity is the sharing of pleasures and pains in common ; 2) The unity of a polis resembles the unity of an individual ; 3) Political unity is the greatest good for a polis.
Aristotle, Pol.
1261 A criticizes the coherence of these claims, arguing that if anything becomes completely unified it becomes an individual and ceases to be a state.
Since the greatest good for a city preserves it, a city cannot be unified as an individual is unified.
R. Mayhew (=> 69-00538) has rightly defended the philosophical significance of Aristotle 's objections.
Reconstruction of the Platonic view shows that a more careful reading avoids the Aristotelian criticisms, and that Aristotle endorses the reconstructed view.
